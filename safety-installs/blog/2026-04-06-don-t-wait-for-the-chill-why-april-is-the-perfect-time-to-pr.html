Don't Wait for the Chill: Why April is the Perfect Time to Prep Your Norfolk Home for Winter

It might seem a little… early to be talking about winter, doesn't it? Here we are in April, the trees are budding, the grass is greening, and the threat of snow feels delightfully distant. But as anyone who’s lived through a few Northeast Nebraska winters knows, the quiet calm of spring is precisely when you should be thinking about the blustery wrath that’s inevitably headed our way. Why now? Because waiting until October to discover your furnace is failing, your pipes are vulnerable, or your roof has a leak is a recipe for a very stressful, very expensive, and very cold winter. Trust me, I’ve seen it happen more times than I can count.

As Justin Watts, owner of Watts Safety Installs here in Norfolk, I’ve spent years helping folks across our 100-mile radius – from Columbus to Sioux City – make their homes safer, more efficient, and ready for whatever Mother Nature throws at us. My team and I often get frantic calls in late fall when people realize they're unprepared. My goal with this post is to help you avoid that panic by giving you a comprehensive checklist of what you can do now, in April, to ensure your home is a warm, safe haven when the snow flies.

Embrace Spring for Exterior Winter Prep: Roof, Gutters, and Foundation First

When the ground thaws and the snow melts, it reveals a lot about how your home handled the previous winter. April is an ideal time to assess and address any damage before summer heat bakes it in, or fall rains exacerbate it. Think of it as a post-mortem for your home’s exterior.

First up, your roof. After a season of heavy snow loads, ice dams, and strong winds, your roof takes a beating. Grab a pair of binoculars or, if you’re comfortable and have the right safety gear, get up on a ladder for a closer look. Are there any missing, cracked, or curled shingles? Are there signs of granular loss? Pay special attention around chimneys, vents, and skylights – these are common areas for leaks to start. Addressing minor roof issues now can prevent major water damage down the line. It's much easier to get a roofer out in April than in October when everyone else is scrambling.

Next, gutters and downspouts. This is an actionable tip you can do yourself right now: clean them out! All that spring rain will quickly expose any clogs from last fall’s leaves and winter debris. Clogged gutters can lead to water overflowing and pooling around your foundation, which is a major no-no. Water around the foundation can seep into your basement, cause cracks, or even lead to structural issues over time. Make sure your downspouts are extended at least 5-6 feet away from your foundation to properly direct water runoff. If you notice any sagging or detached gutters, now’s the time to re-secure or repair them.

Finally, take a walk around your home’s foundation. Look for any new cracks, especially those wider than a credit card. Check for areas where the ground slopes towards your house rather than away from it. Proper grading is crucial for directing water away from your home's vulnerable base. These are the kinds of proactive checks that save you headaches and costly repairs down the road. If you find anything concerning, don't hesitate to reach out for a professional assessment. Sealing the Envelope: Windows, Doors, and Insulation

A significant portion of heat loss in a Nebraska home happens through poorly sealed windows and doors, and inadequate insulation. Addressing these now, in the milder April weather, is a smart move.

This is another actionable tip: go around your house, both inside and out, and check the caulking and weatherstripping around all your windows and doors. Over time, caulk cracks and weatherstripping wears out, creating tiny gaps where cold air can infiltrate and warm air can escape. For older homes common in Norfolk, NE, this is particularly important. A small tube of caulk and a little bit of time can make a huge difference in your energy bill and overall comfort. If you feel a draft when you hold your hand near a window or door frame, you know you’ve found a culprit.
